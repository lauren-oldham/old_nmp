The short excerpt from John Pickles' "A History of Spaces: Cartographic Reason, Mapping and the Geo-Coded World" reminded me of some of the main talking points from last week's reading of the Floating Sheep Manifesto. Particularly of interest is the connection between Pickles' perception of cartography being "dominated by a commitment to modeling a God's-eye view" and the discussion of mappers holding power. Without reading the entire book, it seems like Pickles is implying that what is mapped is a universal truth, which we know is not true. As we all learned in our introductory geography classes, all maps lie. Furthermore, a "God's-eye view" from someone in a position of power is going to look vastly different from the perspective of someone with less power. Two people mapping the same subject area will have completely different perceptions and interpretations of what they're mapping. Does that make any one map "more" true than the other?
